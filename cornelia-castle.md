# Cornelia Castle (FF1) — Harmonic & Melodic Analysis

Key: D major · 4/4 · ~100 BPM · One looping section (~8 bars per pass)
Tracks: MAIN (melody), SUB (arpeggios), BASS, CHORD (reference reduction — derivable from the other three; not an original NES channel)

## Melody Analysis (MAIN track)

### The core finding
**Every downbeat where a new chord arrives lands on a chord tone — zero exceptions in the entire song.**

Favorite landing note: the **3rd** (D→F#, Bm→D, G→B, F#→C#). Root and 5th reserved for resolving/cadence bars.

### Note-by-note, one pass

**D (beats 2–6):** F# (held) · F#, G, F#, E, D
Starts on the 3rd, holds, then scalar walk down. G and E = passing/neighbor tones, each resolved by step immediately.

**A (beats 6–10):** E → A (held ~3 beats)
Pure chord tones, 5th → root.
**Dual-function note:** the landing A is the root of the A chord *and* the 5th of D — melody stays anchored to home even over the V chord. E→A also outlines the A chord itself. (Josh's observation: the melody outlines the opening D chord via F#, never the root, and ends up spending real time on what is the 5th of that first chord.)

**Bm (beats 10–14):** D (held) · D, E, D, C#, B
Exact same shape as the D bar, transposed down a step. Starts on 3rd, walks down, E and C# as passing tones. Confirms the "transposed repeat" A-section pattern.

**F# (beats 14–18):** C# → F# (held)
Same shape as the A bar: 5th → root. So bars 1–4 = one two-bar idea stated twice, second statement transposed and re-harmonized with the secondary dominant.
**Dual-function mirror (Josh):** the landing F# is the root of the F# chord *and* the 5th of the Bm just left — exactly the same relationship as the A note over the A chord (root of A, 5th of D). Bars 3–4 replicate not just the shape of bars 1–2 but the entire harmonic relationship, transposed down a step. One idea, one relationship, stated twice.

**G (beats 18–20):** B (held) · C# (off-beat pickup)
B = 3rd. C# is a pickup targeting the next chord's D.

**Gm (beats 20–22):** D · E (on beat 21)
First downbeat "exception": E = major 6th over a minor chord (Dorian color). Not resolved within the chord — resolves up to F# the moment D arrives. Tension → resolution across the barline.

**D (beats 22–24):** F# · G (on beat 23)
Same device: G on a downbeat, climbing toward the A that lands on the B7.

**B7 (beats 24–26):** A, G, F#
A = 7th of B7 (chord tone on the downbeat). G = passing tone connecting A → F#.

**Em (beats 26–30):** E, B (held), B, C#, D
Root and 5th on downbeats. C#–D = scalar walk-up targeting the F# on beat 30.

**D–A vamp (beats 30–34):** F# (held), E (held)
One chord tone per chord. Loop.

### Where non-chord tones live
1. **Off-beats between chord tones** — passing/neighbor motion, always resolved by step.
2. **Late in a bar as a pickup line** — notes that aren't decorating the current chord but walking toward the *next* chord's downbeat (E over Gm, G over D, C#/D over Em). This is a big part of why the melody always feels like it's going somewhere. **Steal this for B-parts.**

## Arpeggio Track (SUB) — Analysis

Eighth-note pattern, 8 notes per 4-beat chord (4 per 2-beat chord). The 5th sits on every off-beat like a pedal; the interesting motion is in the bottom voice (the on-beat notes).

### First four bars, in scale degrees relative to each chord

| Chord | Pattern | Starts on |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| D  | 5–3–1–3 · 5–3–1–3 | 5th (A3, *below* the root) |
| A  | 3–5–1–5–**2**–5–3–5 | 3rd |
| Bm | 1–5–3–5 · 1–5–3–5 | root (only bar anchored on 1) |
| F# | 3–5–1–5–**2**–5–3–5 | 3rd |

- A and F# bars are **identical patterns, transposed** — same "one idea stated twice" architecture as the melody.
- The **2** in those bars is a passing tone: strip the pedal 5ths and the bottom voice walks 3 → 1 → 2 → 3 (e.g. C#–A–B–C# over the A chord; A#–F#–G#–A# over F#). Chord tones on strong beats, stepwise non-chord tone filling the gap — the arpeggio track follows the same melodic rules as the MAIN track.
- Downbeat starting note differs per chord (5, 3, 1, 3) — arpeggios begin from different chord members, not always the root. This is why it's hard to pick out by ear.

### Complementary motion with the melody (key insight)

The passing-tone walk only happens on the A and F# bars — and those are exactly the bars where the *melody* parks on a long held note. On the D and Bm bars the melody has the busy scalar walk-downs, and the arpeggio stays plain chord tones.

**At any moment, exactly one voice does stepwise melodic motion while the other holds still.** They trade off bar by bar. Classic counterpoint (when one voice moves, the other yields); keeps the texture uncluttered on three monophonic channels; gives the melody room to breathe — the long notes are where the listener digests the phrase while the arpeggio keeps things alive underneath.

**Steal-able:** when the melody holds a long note, put motion in the accompaniment — and vice versa.

### Later bars (to review in detail)

- G: B–G–D–G (3rd on downbeat) → Gm: A#–G–D–G — a single voice changes B→Bb; the whole IV→iv move lives in one note.
- D: D–A–F#–A (1–5–3–5, root position).
- B7: D#–B–B–D# spread across two octaves. Cross-channel spelling: MAIN has A→G→F#, SUB covers D# and B, BASS has B — three monophonic channels jointly spelling a 4-note chord.
- Em: pattern dissolves into a pure descending scale run (B–A–G–F#–E held).
- Turnaround: octave leap + long scale run down (D–D5–C#–B–A–G–F#–E) delivering back to the top of the loop. The arpeggio track abandons arpeggios entirely and becomes a countermelody at the cadence.

## Bass Track — Analysis

One long held note per chord: **D → C# → B → A# (Bb) → G ...** — chromatic stepwise staircase.

### Line-first, not chord-first (key insight)

The bass wasn't written by picking a note out of each chord. The **line was chosen first** — a smooth stepwise descent from D — and the chord voicings (root position vs. first inversion) were chosen to keep the bass on that staircase:

- D chord → D (root)
- A chord → C# (3rd; first inversion, A/C#)
- Bm → B (root)
- F# → A# (3rd; first inversion, F#/A#)

Roots are sacrificed in bars 2 and 4 specifically to preserve the line. **Inversions aren't fancy chord choices — they're what you get when the bass line is the boss.**

### Why a descending stepwise line here

1. **Smoothness** — root-to-root motion (D→A→B→F#) would leap by 4ths/5ths; step motion makes the harmony flow.
2. **Direction/inevitability** — a steady descent feels gently pulled somewhere; right for a stately castle loop.
3. **Tradition** — descending bass from the tonic = the "lament bass" lineage (Dido's Aria, Michelle, Stairway). Ears know this groove.
4. **Counterpoint layering** — melody's overall gesture is also downward, but at eighth-note speed vs. the bass's whole notes, with the arpeggio in the middle. Three layers, three speeds, one shape.

### The A# money note

A# (leading tone to B, the whole reason F# major exists here) appears in **two of three channels at once on the downbeat** — bass holds it, SUB arpeggio hits it first (its "3"). Only the melody avoids it, sailing over on C#/F# (common tones with the surrounding B minor world). Melody stays smooth; inner voices carry the chromatic drama. Watch for money-note doubling at secondary dominants in other Uematsu tracks.

### Practical takeaway for writing bass parts

Don't harmonize chord-by-chord. Sketch where the bass starts, where it should land a few bars later, draw the smoothest line between the points, and let the line dictate the inversions.

### Role framing (for future transcriptions)

- Cornelia Castle bass = **harmonic glue** (long notes, smooth line, makes chords flow).
- Contrast: driving tracks (e.g. Mega Man 2 Wily Stage) = bass as **rhythmic engine**, practically a lead voice. Hypothesis to verify: even galloping basslines are still stepwise *lines* underneath, chopped into repeated rhythmic notes. Appropriateness depends on tempo, energy, and what other voices are doing.

## Middle Stretch (G – Gm – D – B7 – Em) — Analysis

Harmonic rhythm doubles here: two beats per chord (Em gets four).

### Arpeggio patterns (verified)

| Chord | Notes | Degrees |
|-------|-------|---------|
| G  | B–G–D–G | 3–1–5–1 |
| Gm | Bb–G–D–G | b3–1–5–1 |
| D  | D–A–F#–A | 1–5–3–5 |
| B7 | D#–B–B(8vb)–D# | 3–1–1–3 (tritone pair only) |

- Pattern changes from the opening bars: G bars pedal the **root** on off-beats (not the 5th); B7 drops the pedal entirely — just D# and B across two octaves.
- **D# spelling** (not Eb): it's the leading tone of E, rising to E when Em arrives. Spelling follows direction: rising → sharp (A#→B, D#→E); falling → flat (Bb→A).
- **Lead with the information:** the changed/chromatic note goes first in the pattern (B→Bb as the first note of each G bar; D# on the B7 downbeat). The money note gets prime real estate, every time.

### B7 channel allocation — why the arpeggio omits the 5th and 7th

During B7: melody has A→G→F#, arpeggio has D# and B, bass has B. Count the pitch classes: **B (bass+arp), D# (arp), F# (melody), A (melody) — the full root/3rd/5th/7th is present, distributed across channels.** The arpeggio skips A and F# because the melody already has them; doubling wastes a monophonic channel. The one note it cannot skip is D# — the out-of-key leading tone, the reason B7 exists — and nobody else has it.

Note the allocation is the *opposite* of the F# bar: there the melody avoided the money note (A#) and inner voices doubled it; here the melody claims the 7th and 5th, so the arpeggio strips to the tritone pair. **The accompaniment is written around what the melody claims first** — evidence for melody-boss composition.

### The G→Gm bars: three voices, three behaviors

Across beats 18–22:
- **Bass:** frozen — a single G pedal under both chords (verified)
- **Melody:** climbing — B, C#, D, E (never touches Bb)
- **Arpeggio:** carries the one falling harmonic voice — B → Bb

The melody rises and brightens exactly as the harmony darkens: contrary motion is why IV→iv lands bittersweet rather than sad. Practical necessity too: once the arpeggio takes Bb, the melody must avoid B natural (half-step clash between channels) — so it sings B while still legal (over G major), then climbs away; D and E are safe over Gm. The B→Bb handoff happens *between channels*, cleanly.

### Device: isolate the chromatic event

Second confirmed instance of the pattern: when an out-of-key note appears, **one voice carries it, placed prominently, while the other voices clear the stage** (hold still, move elsewhere, or avoid the contested pitch class).
1. F# bar: A# in bass + arpeggio downbeat; melody floats above on common tones.
2. Gm bar: Bb only in the arpeggio, first note of the bar; bass pedals, melody climbs away.

## Closing Unit: B7 → Em → D/A → A → (loop) — Analysis

Josh's hearing: the Em is *part of* the turnaround, not before it — the texture change proves it. The arpeggio switches to descending scale runs exactly at Em and keeps that behavior to the end. Grouping by function: **B7 launches the closing unit; everything from Em onward is one continuous act of getting home.**

**Harmonic formula: ii → I6/4 → V → I** (Em → D/A → A → D-at-restart) — textbook full cadence, with B7 tonicizing the ii to kick it off.

### Plain-language glossary (from this session)
- **Tonicize** = treat a chord as temporary home by preceding it with its own dominant. B7 is V7 of Em → "B7 tonicizes the ii." Same move as F# major → Bm. The target chord isn't altered; the chord *before* it is (D# instead of D).
- **Cadential 6/4** = second-inversion home chord (D/A: 5th in the bass) right before the V. Ear hears it as an A chord with two notes leaning on it — D leans on C#, F# leans on E. Hold the bass A, let both melt down, and it becomes plain A major, then resolves home. Decoration on top of the V that makes the cadence feel earned.

### Track-by-track through the closing unit

**Bass:** E (held 4 beats under Em) → A (held 4 beats under BOTH D/A and A — commits to the dominant early) → D at restart.

**Arpeggio:** abandons arpeggio patterns entirely; becomes a countermelody of descending scale runs:
- Over Em: B–A–G–F#–E(held) — degrees 5–4–3–2–1 in Em
- Over the last two bars: D–D(8va)–C#–B–A–G–F#–E — octave leap, then a full eight-eighth-note run delivering exactly onto the restart downbeat, where the rocking pattern snaps back in.
Two parallel walk-downs, same gesture, second one longer.

**Melody:** near-total stillness — after the C#–D pickup, just **F# (2 beats) → E (2 beats) → F#** (restart downbeat). Neighbor figure; every landing a chord tone (3rd of D, 5th of A, 3rd of D). The F#→E over the held bass A *is* one of the 6/4 leaning resolutions — the melody performs the melting itself. And the turnaround resolves onto the song's opening note: the loop seam is invisible because the last gesture's resolution IS the first note.

**Complementary motion at its most extreme:** arpeggio at its busiest (8-note run) exactly when the melody is at its stillest (two whole notes) — full role swap from the song's opening. The two voices trade the "moving line" job all song; the turnaround is the handoff maximized.

## Form Summary (complete)

| Bars | Function | Chords |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1–4 | A phrase + transposed repeat | D · A · Bm · F# (V/vi) |
| 5–6 | Chromatic pivot (double-time harmony) | G · Gm · D · B7 (V7/ii) |
| 7–8 | Closing unit (launched by B7) | Em · D/A · A → loop |

Skeleton = Pachelbel's Canon / romanesca schema (D–A–Bm–F#(m)–G...), with every deviation a deliberate upgrade: F#m→F# major (secondary dominant), borrowed Gm, B7→Em detour, and inversions converting Pachelbel's leaping bass into a chromatic staircase. Recipe: schema → real melody over it → upgrade the joints.

## Devices Log (running list across transcriptions)

- Secondary dominant (V/vi as F# major; V7/ii as B7)
- Borrowed iv (Gm from parallel minor; IV→iv→I)
- Chromatic bass line cliché (harmonized stepwise descent)
- Transposed-repeat A section (same phrase, down a step, re-harmonized) — recurring Uematsu device across the FF1 OST. Combined with dual-function landing notes, it makes the melody stronger: transposition = instant familiarity, shared tones = seamless connection between chords. Recipe: write two bars, transpose, land on notes shared between adjacent chords.
- Melody: chord tone on every chord-arrival downbeat; 3rd as favorite landing note
- Dual-function melody notes (belong to current + adjacent chord)
- Pickup lines targeting the next chord's downbeat
- Cross-barline resolution (dissonance resolved at the next chord, not within)
- Isolate the chromatic event: one voice carries the out-of-key note, prominently placed; other voices hold, move elsewhere, or vacate the contested pitch class (F# bar, Gm bar)
- Lead with the information: changed/chromatic note first in the pattern, on the strong beat
- Channel allocation flexes per bar: accompaniment covers whichever chord tones the melody doesn't claim; full 7th chords spelled jointly across three monophonic voices
- Enharmonic spelling follows direction: rising → sharp, falling → flat
- Pedal bass under a chord change (G held across G→Gm)
- Contrary motion at emotional pivots (melody climbs while harmony darkens)
- Full cadence closing formula: ii → I6/4 → V → I, launched by a secondary dominant
- Texture change marks section boundaries (arpeggio pattern → scale runs at the closing unit)
- Loop seam welding: turnaround's resolution note = song's opening note; scale run delivers onto the restart downbeat
- Trade-off maximized at cadence: busiest accompaniment under stillest melody
